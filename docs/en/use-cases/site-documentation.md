# Use Case Guide: Site Documentation

> **UC1** | For Facility Managers, Operations teams, and multi-site managers
> Estimated reading time: 12 minutes

---

## 1. Who This Guide Is For

This guide is for professionals who manage physical spaces across one or multiple locations and need a reliable, repeatable way to document building conditions, asset states, or site status over time.

Typical roles:

- **Facility Managers** overseeing a portfolio of buildings (offices, warehouses, retail stores)
- **Operations teams** responsible for site readiness, layout changes, or equipment tracking
- **Multi-site managers** who cannot visit every location every week but still need to make informed decisions remotely
- **QHSE officers** maintaining visual records for compliance and safety audits

If your current process relies on smartphone photos stored in WhatsApp threads, email attachments, or shared drives with inconsistent folder structures, this guide will show you a better way.

---

## 2. The Challenge

Most organizations document their sites using 2D photos taken on smartphones. These images end up scattered across personal devices, messaging apps, and cloud folders with no consistent naming or structure.

The consequences are predictable:

- **No spatial context.** A flat photo of a damaged ceiling tells you nothing about which room, which floor, or which building you are looking at.
- **No historical record.** When you need to compare current conditions to last quarter, the photos are buried somewhere or deleted entirely.
- **Sharing is painful.** Briefing a contractor means describing the space verbally or sending a dozen unrelated photos. Misunderstandings lead to unnecessary site visits.
- **Decisions are delayed.** Without reliable visual data, managers travel to sites just to understand the situation before they can act.

RICOH360 with a Business Pack gives you a structured, 360-degree documentation workflow that solves each of these problems.

## 3. Recommended Setup

### Choosing the right camera

The right camera depends on how your team will capture and what conditions they work in.

**Start here: Will non-technical operators use Simple Capture (Type S)?**
Simple Capture lets the operator select an album directly on the camera screen, press the button, and have photos auto-upload to the cloud. No phone needed, no app interaction. If this is your workflow, **THETA X is required**. Simple Capture is not available on the THETA A1.

**Will your team primarily capture video walkthroughs?**
If long video recordings are part of the workflow (e.g., walkthrough documentation of large buildings), the **THETA A1** has a significant advantage with up to 120 minutes of video recording, compared to roughly 25 minutes on the THETA X. For panoramic photo capture, which is the primary mode for most site documentation, both cameras perform equally well.

**Are your sites dusty, wet, or exposed to extreme temperatures?**
The **THETA A1** carries an IP64 rating (dust-tight, splash-resistant) and operates from -10C to 40C. The THETA X has no IP rating. For warehouses with controlled environments or office buildings, this is not a factor. For outdoor yards, industrial facilities, or unheated storage areas in winter, it matters.

For a full feature comparison, see: [Camera Comparison](/en/reference/camera-comparison)

### Choosing your accessories

Pick based on how your team moves through spaces.

- **TD-2 (tripod/monopod hybrid):** Provides a stable base for floor placement. Best for fixed-point captures where you need the camera to stand by itself at a consistent position, such as virtual tours or repeat-visit documentation of the same rooms. Heavier to carry.
- **TM-2 (monopod/selfie stick):** Lightweight and designed for carrying. Best for walkthroughs where you move between rooms and areas quickly. Faster to deploy. Can be combined with the helmet mount adapter for hands-free capture.
- **Both TD-2 and TM-2:** Recommended when your team needs fixed-point captures in some areas and mobile walkthroughs in others during the same site visit.
- **Helmet mount (TM-2 adapter):** Attaches to a hard hat for hands-free capture while walking. Useful in environments where both hands must stay free.

Choose based on your workflow. There is no single best option.

### Album structure

Albums are your primary organizational tool. For site documentation, structure them by location and, optionally, by capture campaign.

**Recommended naming convention:**

```
[Site Name] - [Zone or Building] - [Date or Quarter]
```

Examples:
- `Lyon Warehouse - Dock Area - 2026-Q1`
- `Paris HQ - Floor 2 - March 2026`
- `Rungis Site - Exterior - Initial Capture`

Keep your album count manageable. If you use a Business Pack Type S (Simple Capture), the camera screen works best with 10 to 15 albums. Beyond that, navigation on the device becomes cumbersome.

> **Go further**
> - Album settings and structure: [BP Type S album settings](https://support.ricoh360.com/fr/tags/bp-type-s-album-settings)

### Capture frequency

Choose a rhythm that matches your operational needs:

| Frequency | Best for |
|-----------|----------|
| **Monthly** | Rapidly changing environments (warehouses, retail resets) |
| **Quarterly** | Stable sites where you track gradual wear or seasonal changes |
| **Event-driven** | Before/after renovations, incident documentation, pre-audit records |

Consistency matters more than frequency. Capturing from the same positions each time is what makes historical comparison valuable.

## 4. Capture Workflow

### Before the visit

1. **Prepare your albums.** In the RICOH360 web app, create or verify the albums you will use. If you are on a Type S pack, assign the relevant albums to your THETA X camera from the admin console.
2. **Charge the camera.** A full charge on the THETA X gives you enough battery for a typical site visit. For large sites, bring a USB-C cable for top-up charging.
3. **Plan your route.** Decide in advance which rooms or zones you will capture. A consistent route (same order each visit) makes it much easier to compare captures over time.

> **Go further**
> - Uploading and transfer: [BP Type S upload](https://support.ricoh360.com/fr/tags/bp-type-s-upload)
> - MasterClass Video 3/14, Upload workflow: [Watch on YouTube (FR)](https://youtu.be/A0VD6f5HSRQ)

### During the visit

1. **Select the right album** on your camera screen (Type S) or mobile app (Core pack).
2. **Capture systematically.** Work room by room, following the same path every time.
   - Hold the camera at a **consistent height** (chest to eye level, roughly 1.2 to 1.6 meters). This ensures panoramas are comparable across visits.
   - Stand in the **center of the room** when possible for the best 360-degree coverage.
   - In corridors or large spaces, capture every 3 to 5 meters to ensure full coverage without gaps.
3. **Wait for the capture confirmation** before moving. The camera needs a moment to process, especially in HDR mode.
4. **Note any issues** you want to annotate later (a damaged wall, a blocked exit, equipment out of place).

### After the visit

1. **Verify the upload.** Once back on WiFi, the THETA X transfers photos automatically to the cloud. Check your album in the RICOH360 web app to confirm all captures arrived.
2. **Review in the web app.** Open each panorama in the viewer to confirm image quality. Look for blurring (camera movement during capture) or underexposed images.
3. **Add annotations** to flag specific items: damage, compliance issues, or notes for contractors.

## 5. Organize and Navigate

### Albums for grouping

Albums are the backbone of your organization. Each album can represent a site, a zone within a site, or a specific capture campaign. You can browse albums in grid or list view in the web app.

A media file lives in the global gallery and can be added to one or more albums. Deleting an album does not delete the underlying photos.

> **Feature highlight: Albums [FREE]** -- Albums are the foundation for organizing captures by site, building, or campaign. Every Business Pack includes album management at no extra cost.

For more on album workflows, see: [Organizing with Albums](/en/daily-use/organizing-with-albums)

### Projects and floor plans for spatial navigation

If you have floor plans (PNG, JPG, or SVG), you can create a Project and upload the plans as background images. Then place markers (pins) on the plan to link each position to its corresponding 360 panorama.

This turns your documentation into a navigable map: click a pin on the plan, see the full 360-degree view from that exact spot.

**When to use Projects vs. Albums:**

| Situation | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| You have floor plans available | Projects with markers for spatial navigation |
| You do not have floor plans | Albums are sufficient and fully functional |
| You manage many sites without plans | Start with albums, add Projects later when plans become available |

Note: Projects require floor plans to be useful. Without an uploaded plan, the project view is empty. Markers must be positioned manually on the plan (there is no automatic GPS placement).

For more on Projects, see: [Projects and Floor Plans](/en/premium-features/projects-and-floor-plans)

### AI enhancement for difficult conditions

Some spaces are hard to photograph well: basements, mechanical rooms, storage areas with poor lighting.

> **Feature highlight: AI Enhancement [FREE]** -- Improves brightness and resolution on underexposed or dark panoramas. Particularly valuable for basements, utility rooms, and any space where lighting is poor. Processed in the cloud, available directly from the viewer.

- **Enhancement** improves brightness and resolution on underexposed or dark panoramas. This is processed in the cloud and available directly from the viewer.
- **Super Resolution** upscales image detail for zooming into specific areas (useful for reading labels, serial numbers, or signage in panoramas).

> **Go further**
> - MasterClass Video 8/14, Super Resolution: [Watch on YouTube (FR)](https://youtu.be/PUrD701fmRw)

### Before/after with Side-by-Side

> **Feature highlight: Side-by-Side Comparison [FREE]** -- Display two panoramas next to each other for before/after comparison. Essential for tracking changes across quarterly visits, documenting renovation progress, or comparing pre-incident and post-incident conditions.

For the comparison workflow, see: [Side-by-Side Comparison](/en/daily-use/side-by-side-comparison)

## 6. Collaborate and Share

### Internal collaboration

RICOH360 is designed for team-based workflows, not individual use.

- **Team invitation [PREMIUM]:** Invite up to 10 colleagues per Business Pack license. Team members get full access to albums, projects, annotations, and the viewer.
- **Shared albums:** All team members can browse and contribute to the same albums. No need to send files or links back and forth.
- **Annotations for flagging issues [PREMIUM]:** Place annotations directly on panoramas to flag damage, compliance issues, or items needing attention. Other team members see these annotations in context, reducing back-and-forth communication.
- **Projects for spatial context [PREMIUM]:** When team members open a project, they navigate the floor plan and click on markers to see panoramas. This gives remote colleagues the same spatial understanding as someone who was physically on site.

### External collaboration

Share 360-degree captures with people outside your team (contractors, clients, insurers, auditors):

- **Public link sharing:** Generate a shareable link for an individual panorama. The recipient can view the 360-degree image in their browser without needing a RICOH360 account.
- **What external viewers see:** The full 360-degree panorama with navigation. They can pan, zoom, and explore the space.
- **What external viewers do not see:** Annotations are stripped from shared links. Project views (floor plans with markers) are not accessible via shared links.

For broad or recurring access, invite key external stakeholders as team members instead.

For details on sharing workflows, see: [Sharing with External](/en/daily-use/sharing-with-external)

### Using 360 in meetings

Open the RICOH360 web app on a shared screen during team meetings, client calls, or contractor briefings. Navigate the site in real time: participants can ask "show me the loading dock" or "what does the east wall look like?" and you pan to it instantly. This replaces the need to flip through dozens of flat photos and is far more effective for remote site walks.

### Privacy compliance

If your site documentation captures people (employees, visitors), the automatic face blur feature detects and blurs faces in panoramas. This helps with privacy compliance without requiring manual editing.

> **Go further**
> - MasterClass Video 9/14, Face blur: [Watch on YouTube (FR)](https://youtu.be/VXNK0fQgK4c)
> - Image viewing features: [BP Type S viewing](https://support.ricoh360.com/fr/tags/bp-type-s-viewing)

### Limitations to know

Be aware of these current platform boundaries so you can plan accordingly:

- **Sharing is per-panorama.** You cannot share an entire album or project via a single link. For broad access, invite stakeholders as team members instead.
- **Annotations are not visible in shared links.** External recipients see the 360-degree image but not your annotations. If annotations are critical, share via team invitation.
- **Projects cannot be shared externally.** Floor plans with markers are only visible to team members, not through public links.
- **Side-by-Side comparison is manual.** There is no automatic date-based selector. You choose which two panoramas to compare yourself.

For the full list, see: [Known Limitations](/en/troubleshooting/known-limitations)

## 7. Tips from the Field

### Best practices for consistent documentation

- **Same route, every time.** Document rooms in the same order at each visit. This makes it much easier to find and compare captures across periods.
- **Name albums with dates.** Including the quarter or month in the album name (`Q1-2026`, `March 2026`) prevents confusion when you have multiple capture campaigns for the same site.
- **Capture at consistent height.** A selfie stick or monopod set to a fixed height (around 1.4 meters) ensures panoramas look comparable. Avoid handheld captures at varying heights.
- **Capture with purpose.** Before pressing the button, ask: "What do I want this panorama to show?" Center yourself where the most important elements are visible.
- **Review the same day.** Check your uploads in the web app the same day you capture. Spotting a blurred or missing panorama while the visit is fresh saves a return trip.

### Common mistakes to avoid

- **Too many albums on Simple Capture.** Keep it under 15. The camera screen is not built for scrolling through long lists.
- **Inconsistent naming.** "Site Rungis Photos" and "Rungis Q1" and "Warehouse Rungis New" will create confusion within weeks. Pick a naming convention and stick with it.
- **Moving while capturing.** The THETA X needs a still moment to capture, especially in HDR mode. Walk to the position, stop, hold still, capture, then move.
- **Forgetting the "before" capture.** If a renovation or change is planned, capture the current state before work begins. You will need that baseline for comparison.
- **Skipping dark spaces.** Basements and utility rooms matter. Capture them anyway and use Enhancement to improve the image quality after upload.
