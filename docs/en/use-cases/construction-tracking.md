# UC2: Construction Progress Tracking with RICOH360 Business Pack

A practical guide for project managers and site supervisors who need to track construction progress, document trade handovers, and prevent disputes using 360-degree capture.

---

## 1. Who This Guide Is For

This guide is built for professionals managing active construction or renovation sites:

- **Project Managers** coordinating multiple trades across weeks or months
- **Site Supervisors** conducting regular site walks and reporting progress
- **General Contractors** documenting milestones for clients and architects
- **Subcontractors** needing to prove scope completion before handover

If you are already familiar with construction workflows but new to 360-degree documentation, this is your starting point. No BIM model required. No prior 360 experience needed.

---

## 2. The Challenge

Most construction teams still rely on 2D smartphone photos, emailed to stakeholders with minimal context. This creates a set of recurring problems:

- **Schedule disputes.** When a trade claims work was completed by a certain date, there is often no reliable visual proof.
- **Rework claims.** Damage caused by a follow-on trade is hard to attribute without timestamped evidence of what the space looked like before they entered.
- **Poor visibility for remote stakeholders.** Clients, architects, and project owners who are not on site regularly struggle to understand progress from a handful of flat photos.
- **Wasted site visits.** Someone asks "what does the ceiling look like in room 204?" and the answer requires a physical trip back to site.

360-degree capture changes this dynamic. A single panorama from the center of a room documents every wall, every corner, every detail. Timestamped, organized, and accessible from anywhere.

## 3. Recommended Setup

### Choosing the right camera

Construction sites have specific requirements. Here is how to decide.

**Will non-technical operators use Simple Capture (Type S)?**
If site workers or supervisors need to select an album on the camera screen, press the button, and have everything auto-upload without a phone, **THETA X is required**. Simple Capture is not available on the THETA A1.

**Is video walkthrough a primary capture mode?**
For long video walkthroughs of floors or building sections, the **THETA A1** offers up to 120 minutes of continuous video recording. The THETA X caps at roughly 25 minutes. If your main capture mode is panoramic photos (the standard for weekly progress tracking), both cameras work equally well.

**Is the site dusty, wet, or subject to extreme temperatures?**
Active construction sites often involve concrete dust, open-air exposure, and winter conditions. The **THETA A1** is IP64-rated (dust-tight, splash-resistant) and operates from -10C to 40C. The THETA X has no IP rating. For interior renovation with controlled conditions, this may not matter. For structural phases or outdoor work, it does.

For a full feature comparison, see: [Camera Comparison](/en/reference/camera-comparison)

### Choosing your accessories

Construction teams often need different setups for different tasks on the same site visit.

- **TD-2 (tripod/monopod hybrid):** Stable floor placement for fixed-point captures. Best for documenting the same positions every week (rooms, junctions, key areas). The consistent height and position makes Side-by-Side comparison much more effective over time. Heavier to carry around.
- **TM-2 (monopod/selfie stick):** Lightweight and fast to deploy. Best for moving through floors quickly, capturing multiple rooms in sequence. Can be combined with the helmet mount adapter for hands-free use.
- **Both TD-2 and TM-2:** Recommended for construction teams. Use the TD-2 for your fixed weekly positions and the TM-2 for walkthrough coverage of new areas or ad-hoc captures.
- **Helmet mount (TM-2 adapter):** Attaches to a hard hat for hands-free 360 capture while walking the site. Particularly useful when both hands are needed (climbing ladders, holding plans, pointing at defects during a site walk).

### Albums: your timeline

Create one Album per capture session or milestone. Albums act as your chronological backbone.

Examples:
- `Week 12`, `Week 13`, `Week 14`...
- `Phase 1: Structural`, `Phase 2: MEP Rough-in`, `Phase 3: Finishing`
- `Plumbing Handover`, `Electrical Handover`

**Practical note:** Simple Capture on the THETA X displays albums directly on the camera screen. This works well for up to 10-15 albums. If your project will generate more, use a naming convention that keeps the most recent albums easy to find (e.g., prefix with dates).

### Projects with floor plans: your spatial map

If you have floor plans (PDF, PNG, or JPG of your architectural drawings), create a Project in the RICOH360 App. Upload your plans as floor plan images, then position capture markers on the plan to show exactly where each panorama was taken.

> **Feature highlight: Projects and Floor Plans [PREMIUM]** -- Turn your floor plans into interactive maps. Click a pin, see the 360-degree view from that spot. Essential for multi-floor sites where spatial context is as important as the visual record itself.

This is particularly valuable when:
- Your site has multiple floors or zones
- Remote stakeholders need to understand spatial context
- You want to compare the same position across multiple weeks

**Important:** Projects require a floor plan to be useful. If you do not have plans available, Albums alone are sufficient to get started.

### Capture cadence

For most active construction sites, a **weekly capture** is the right rhythm. For critical phases (concrete pours, waterproofing, insulation before closing walls), consider capturing before and after key milestones.

## 4. Weekly Capture Workflow

### Before the site visit

1. Open the RICOH360 App and review the previous week's captures
2. Identify areas where progress should be visible this week
3. Plan your route: which rooms, which floors, in what order
4. Charge the THETA X camera fully (see battery tips in Section 8)

### During the capture

1. **Create or select the album** for this week (e.g., `Week 14`)
2. **Work room by room**, systematically. Start at one end of the site and move through in a consistent order
3. **Use the same positions each week.** Stand in the same spot in each room for every capture session. This makes Side-by-Side comparison dramatically more useful later
4. **Hold the camera at a consistent height** (roughly eye level, arm extended above your head). A selfie stick or monopod helps maintain consistency
5. **Step away from the camera** if possible, or stay still during capture to reduce your presence in the panorama

> **Go further:** Tips for indoor and outdoor captures (FR): https://youtu.be/ml36-En7UwM

### After the capture

1. Ensure all panoramas have uploaded to the cloud (verify in the RICOH360 App gallery)
2. If using Projects, position new markers on the floor plan
3. Use **Side-by-Side comparison** to compare this week's captures with the previous week

## 5. Track Progress Over Time

### Side-by-Side comparison

> **Feature highlight: Side-by-Side Comparison [FREE]** -- Display two panoramas next to each other for week-over-week progress tracking. Each panel navigates independently. The single most useful feature for construction teams tracking weekly changes.

The Side-by-Side view places two panoramas next to each other, each with independent 360-degree navigation. This is the core tool for tracking progress.

Typical uses:
- Compare the same room between Week 12 and Week 14
- Show a client what changed since their last site visit
- Verify that remediation work was actually completed

**How it works:** Open any panorama in the Viewer, activate the Side-by-Side mode, then select the comparison panorama from the filmstrip. Each panel navigates independently.

**Limitation to know:** There is no automatic date-based selector. You choose the comparison panorama manually. Consistent album naming (by week or date) makes this much easier.

For the comparison workflow, see: [Side-by-Side Comparison](/en/daily-use/side-by-side-comparison)

> **Go further:** Viewer walkthrough (FR): https://youtu.be/adu_IuLef2M

### Annotations

> **Feature highlight: Annotations [PREMIUM]** -- Flag defects, incomplete work, or punch list items directly on panoramas. Each annotation is positioned as a hotspot in the 360-degree view, creating a visual map of all findings. Visible to all team members.

Use Annotations to flag specific issues directly on a panorama:
- Mark a defect (crack, water damage, incomplete work)
- Note a delay or blocked area
- Document a punch list item

Each annotation has a title and description, positioned as a hotspot in the 360-degree view.

**Limitation to know:** Annotations are visible only to team members logged into RICOH360. They are not included when sharing a panorama via a public link. Plan accordingly for external stakeholder communication (see Section 6).

For annotation details, see: [Annotations and Markup](/en/daily-use/annotations-and-markup)

### Albums as timeline

Your album structure becomes your project timeline. When a question arises about "what did the third floor look like in March?", you navigate to the relevant album and find the answer in seconds.

## 6. Collaborate and Share

### Internal collaboration

Construction projects involve multiple people who need access to the same visual record.

- **Team invitation [PREMIUM]:** Invite up to 10 colleagues per Business Pack license. Project managers, site supervisors, and key team members all get full access to albums, projects, annotations, and the viewer.
- **Shared albums:** Everyone on the team sees the same album structure and the same captures. No more emailing photos or sharing Dropbox links.
- **Annotations for defect tracking [PREMIUM]:** When a supervisor flags a defect in an annotation, the project manager sees it immediately in the viewer. No separate reporting needed for internal issue tracking.
- **Projects for remote oversight [PREMIUM]:** Team members who are not on site can open the project, navigate the floor plan, and click through to panoramas. This gives remote stakeholders meaningful visibility without requiring a site visit.

### External collaboration

Share 360-degree captures with clients, architects, inspectors, and other parties outside your team:

- **Public link sharing:** Generate a shareable link for an individual panorama. The recipient views the full 360-degree image in their browser, no account needed.
- **What external viewers see:** The complete panorama with pan and zoom navigation.
- **What external viewers do not see:** Annotations are stripped. Floor plan views (projects) are not accessible. Sharing is per individual panorama, not per album or project.

**Workaround:** For important external communications, take a screenshot of the annotated view and share it alongside the 360 link. Or invite key external stakeholders as team members for full access.

### Using 360 in meetings

During coordination meetings or client progress reviews, open the RICOH360 web app on a shared screen and navigate the site in real time. This is far more effective than flipping through 2D photos. Participants can ask "show me the northwest corner" and you can pan to it instantly. For weekly coordination meetings with trades, this becomes a natural way to walk through the site virtually and align on priorities.

## 7. Handle Disputes and Claims

This is where 360-degree documentation pays for itself.

### Timestamped evidence

Every panorama in RICOH360 carries a capture timestamp. When a subcontractor claims work was completed by a specific date, you can verify it against the panorama captured that week.

### Before/after proof

Side-by-Side comparison provides clear visual evidence of change (or lack of change) between two dates. This is useful for:
- Proving that damage occurred after a specific trade entered the area
- Showing that a defect existed before a certain phase started
- Demonstrating progress (or delays) to a client or insurer

### Trade handover documentation

Create a dedicated album for each trade handover (e.g., `Handover: Plumbing to Drywall`). Capture the full state of every room before the next trade begins. This album becomes the agreed baseline. If damage occurs afterward, the evidence is already documented.

## 8. Tips from the Field

These insights come from the Buildwise POC, where 26 construction professionals across Belgium tested THETA X cameras and the RICOH360 platform on active sites.

### Low-light captures

Basements, unfinished areas without electrical service, and enclosed spaces are common on construction sites. The THETA X handles moderate low light reasonably well, but very dark spaces produce noisy images.

**Recommendations:**
- Use HDR mode for any indoor space without full lighting
- Bring a portable LED panel for truly dark areas (basements, mechanical rooms)
- Accept that some captures will be darker than ideal. The documentation value still outweighs the image quality limitation

> **Go further:** Low-light capture tips (FR): https://youtu.be/Qfx7qdT8DXs

### Battery management

Buildwise users flagged battery life as a practical concern during full-day site visits covering multiple floors.

**Recommendations:**
- A fully charged THETA X supports approximately 150-200 captures
- For large sites, carry a USB-C power bank and recharge between floors
- Turn off the camera between capture zones rather than leaving it idle

### Annotation limitations

Buildwise testers noted that while annotations are valuable for internal issue tracking, the fact that they are not visible to external viewers via shared links limits their usefulness for multi-party communication. Plan your sharing workflow with this constraint in mind.

### Upload speed

On sites with limited connectivity, cloud upload can be slow. Consider:
- Uploading over WiFi back at the office rather than on site
- Using mobile hotspot if site WiFi is unreliable
- Checking the upload queue in the mobile app before leaving the site
